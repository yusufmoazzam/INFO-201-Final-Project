Human trafficking is a global issue that affects communities in many different ways, and it’s often connected to broader patterns of crime and social vulnerability. According to the United Nation Office on Drugs and Crime, human trafficking involves the exploitation of people through force, fraud, and coercion and occurs in many forms, including forced labor and commercial sexual exploitation. While trafficking is often studied as a standalone issue, it may also be influenced by other social factors such as crime levels and population size. Understanding how trafficking relates to other crime patterns across the United States can help provide insight into where trafficking is more likely to occur and how it may interact with broader crime environments. More background on human trafficking can be found here: https://www.unodc.org/unodc/en/human-trafficking/what-is-human-trafficking.html

This project focuses on three main research questions: How do the rates of either property or violent crimes correlate with the Human Trafficking rates in each state? How does population size affect the number of reported human trafficking cases around all states? Is there a relationship between violent crime rates and property crime rates across the U.S.? These questions allow us to examine different dimensions of crime across states and see whether human trafficking appears to follow similar patterns as other criminal activity. By analyzing these relationships, we hope to identify whether states with higher crime levels or larger populations tend to report higher numbers of trafficking cases.

To answer these questions, we combined two different datasets. The first dataset contains U.S. state crime statistics, including violent crime rates, property crime rates, and population estimates. This dataset is available through the CORGIS dataset collection and can be accessed here: https://corgis-edu.github.io/corgis/csv/state_crime/. The second dataset contains information on reported human trafficking incidents by law enforcement agencies across the United States between 2013 and 2019. This dataset was obtained from the Inter-University Consortium for Political and Social Research (ICPSR) data archive and is available here: https://www.openicpsr.org/openicpsr/project/117974/version/V3/view?path=/openicpsr/117974/fcr:versions/V3/human_trafficking_2013_2019_csv.zip&type=file/. After downloading the datasets, we filtered them to include the years 2015-2019 and merged them so that each observation represents a state in a given year.

Analyzing these relationships is important because it can help reveal patterns between human trafficking and other crime indicators across the United States. If trafficking tends to occur more frequently in states with higher crime rates or larger populations, this information could help researchers and policymakers better understand where prevention efforts may be most needed. However, there are also important limitations and ethical considerations when using these datasets. Human trafficking is widely believed to be underreported, meaning the number of cases likely underestimates the true scale of the problem. Additionally, reporting practices may vary between law enforcement agencies, which can affect the consistency of the data. Because of these limitations, our analysis should be interpreted as identifying patterns in reported cases rather than providing a complete measure of trafficking activity.

This project explored how human trafficking relates to other crime patterns and population differences across U.S. states. One important takeaway from our analysis is that states with larger populations tend to report more human trafficking cases. This pattern makes sense because states with more people naturally have more crimes occur and be reported. However, population size alone does not fully explain trafficking patterns, and other social or economic factors may also play a role.

Another key takeaway is the relationship between property crime and violent crime rates across states. Our visualization shows a positive relationship between the two, meaning that states with higher property crime rates often also have higher violent crime rates. This suggests that different types of crime may occur in similar environments or be influenced by similar conditions such as poverty or policing practices.

It is important to recognize that our analysis likely underestimates the true number of human trafficking cases. Human trafficking is widely believed to be underreported, and many victims may never be identified or reported to law enforcement. Because of this, the dataset only reflects reported incidents rather than the true scale of trafficking. Future research could explore additional data sources, such as nonprofit reports or victim support organizations, to better understand trafficking patterns and improve estimates of where trafficking may be occurring.
